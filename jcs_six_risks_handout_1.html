Six Risks That Can Break Any Retirement Plan
Regardless of how well you've prepared — these are the gaps most plans don't account for.
This is not a sales document. It's a diagnostic. Read through each risk, check the ones that apply to your situation, and use it as a starting point for an honest conversation about where your plan is strong — and where it might be exposed.
Tap or click each checkbox that applies to your situation. Your score at the bottom will show which risks are unaddressed — and how urgent the conversation is.
1
Longevity Risk
Outliving your savings due to living longer than expected
The data at 65: 34% of people reaching age 65 will live to 90. 12% will reach 95 or beyond. The average retiree at 65 has 19.5 years of retirement ahead — and that's the average. Half will live longer. Every additional year costs money your plan may not have modeled.
What it looks like in real life
You run out of money at 88. Social Security continues, but the assets you planned to live on are gone. You haven't done anything wrong — you just lived. Most retirement plans are modeled to age 85 or 87. A plan designed to 95 looks very different from one designed to 85.
Check what applies to you
My income plan is designed to last to age 95 or beyond
At least one of my income sources is guaranteed for life regardless of how long I live
I have a plan for the "slow-go" and "no-go" years — not just the active early retirement years
2
Market Volatility Risk
A major downturn early in retirement that you can't recover from
The data: The S&P 500 averages 2 to 2.5 years to recover prior highs after a significant downturn. Losing 30% in Year 2 of retirement and withdrawing to fund living expenses permanently locks in those losses. This is called sequence-of-returns risk — and it hits hardest at exactly the wrong time.
What it looks like in real life
The market drops 35% the year after you retire. You need $60,000 to live on. You sell shares at the bottom to raise the cash. When the market recovers, you own fewer shares than before — so you recover less. You repeat this for two more years. The portfolio never fully comes back.
Check what applies to you
I have a source of income or liquidity completely separate from my investment portfolio
If the market dropped 30% tomorrow, I could cover 2+ years of expenses without selling investments
My plan has been stress-tested against a 2008-level event in Year 1 or 2 of retirement
3
Inflation Risk
Rising costs that quietly erode what your money can actually buy
The data: At a historical average of 3% annually, $50,000 of purchasing power today requires approximately $90,500 in 20 years to buy the same things. Fixed income sources — pensions, annuities, Social Security cost-of-living adjustments — may not keep pace. What feels comfortable at 65 can feel tight at 80.
What it looks like in real life
Your pension covers your expenses at 67. By 78, groceries, utilities, insurance, and healthcare have all risen while your pension payment has stayed the same. You start drawing down savings to fill the gap — money you planned to leave for your family or keep for emergencies.
Check what applies to you
Some portion of my assets is still growing to offset rising costs over time
My income plan accounts for costs being significantly higher in 15–20 years
I have at least one income source that adjusts with inflation or grows over time
4
Tax Risk
Future tax rates and forced withdrawals that take more than you planned
The data: Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) begin at age 73 and force taxable withdrawals from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s whether you need the money or not. Current federal tax rates are historically low — the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act provisions expire in 2025. The U.S. national debt suggests rates are unlikely to go down long-term.
What it looks like in real life
At 73, you're forced to withdraw $80,000 from your IRA even though you don't need it. This pushes your income into a higher bracket, increases your Medicare premiums through IRMAA surcharges, and potentially makes more of your Social Security taxable. You pay taxes on money you weren't planning to spend.
Check what applies to you
I have a plan to reduce my tax-deferred balance before RMDs begin at 73
I have at least one tax-free income source I can draw from in high-income years
I've modeled what my RMDs look like at 75, 80, and 85 — not just today
5
Healthcare & Long-Term Care Risk
A care event that can deplete decades of savings in just a few years
The data: 41% of men and 51% of women over 65 will need paid long-term care. 35% will enter a nursing home. In Portland/Vancouver: nursing home costs run $9,000–$12,000/month ($108,000–$144,000/year). Memory care: $6,000–$9,000/month. In-home care: $30–$43/hour. A 5-year care event can cost $500,000–$720,000 — from a single portfolio.
What it looks like in real life
Your spouse needs memory care at $7,500/month. You spend $90,000 per year from your savings for four years. That's $360,000 gone — before you account for your own living expenses during that period, your own future care needs, or what you hoped to leave your family. The portfolio you spent 40 years building is cut in half in less than five years.
Check what applies to you
I have a specific plan — not just savings — for a multi-year long-term care event
My LTC plan protects my spouse's finances if I need care first
My plan for LTC doesn't require me to be healthy and cognitively intact to activate it
6
Legacy Risk
Unintentional erosion of what you planned to pass on to family
The data: Traditional IRA and 401(k) assets passed to heirs are subject to ordinary income tax — your heirs must withdraw within 10 years under current rules (SECURE Act 2.0), potentially pushing them into higher brackets. Assets consumed by LTC costs, taxes, or market losses reduce what actually transfers. Estate shrinkage is often silent and unplanned.
What it looks like in real life
You leave $800,000 in a traditional IRA to your children. They're required to withdraw it within 10 years. Between their own income and the forced IRA distributions, they pay 32–37% in federal tax on much of it. The $800,000 you built becomes $520,000 after taxes. No one made a mistake — the tax bill was always there, waiting.
Check what applies to you
I know the actual after-tax value of what my heirs will receive — not just the account balance
My estate plan accounts for the tax bill my heirs will inherit along with my assets
I have at least one tax-free asset that passes without a tax burden attached to it
